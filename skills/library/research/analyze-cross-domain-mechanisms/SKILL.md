# Analyze Cross-Domain Mechanisms

## Start with two evidence spines

Identify at least two independently sourced domains. For each, state the observed phenomenon, population, time scale, incentives, constraints, and source limitations. Similar vocabulary or narrative shape is not independent evidence.

## Isolate the transferable mechanism

Describe the causal chain in neutral terms: inputs, actors, constraint or incentive, transformation, output, and feedback. Then map each link separately into both domains. Mark any link supported only by analogy.

When strategic interaction is present, test incentives, coordination, signaling, repeated behavior, proxy metrics, likely equilibrium, Goodhart pressure, failure mode, and plausible countermeasure. Do not add strategic language when the system has no adapting actors.

## Try to break the bridge

Require:

- one falsifiable prediction in the target domain;
- the strongest rival explanation;
- a counterexample or failed transfer;
- boundary conditions and non-applicability;
- a constructive counterweight when critique is one-sided; and
- the next domain-specific observation needed.

Creative or historical material may generate a useful hypothesis, but empirical promotion requires independent contact with the target domain. If the mechanism does not survive, record a non-connection rather than weakening the standard.

## Produce only useful outputs

Return the source handles, mechanism map, confidence, falsifier, non-applicability, and disposition. Create a durable bridge or pattern note only when it changes a project, experiment, question, or decision; otherwise leave a compact research lead or `no-edge` result.


## When no edge survives

When the analysis finds no supported mechanism, advantage, or connection, the disposition is `no-edge` — the finding — not `kill`; `kill` is reserved for rejecting a governed proposal or mechanism that was formally under admission.
